01
The skeptic asks
"How do I know you're not just confirming what you already think?"

The independence rule does the work.

Two sources only count as independent if they obtained their information through genuinely different means. Five outlets republishing the same Reuters report counts as one source, not five. This is how the system avoids confusing reach with corroboration.

The consensus exception is bounded.

When five or more outlets consistently report the same claim without contradiction, the claim can be assigned at reduced confidence — not full confidence. This is a guardrail against echo chambers, not an endorsement of them.

Outlet claims vs attributed claims.

A claim made directly by the outlet is scored against the outlet. A claim attributed to a named speaker — a politician, an official, a CEO — is scored against the speaker's record, not the outlet's. The outlet is evaluated on whether it quoted accurately.

Worked example · hypothetical claim
Sentinel Press · April 2026
"The Senate passed the Western Water Security Act 61 to 38, allocating $4.2 billion over seven years to permanently resolve interstate water disputes."
Step 01
Ingestion
Article fetched. Source identified. Publication timestamp parsed. Article is older than the 6-hour gate, so any verdicts produced will affect the outlet's live score.
Step 02
Extraction
Three checkable factual claims extracted from the sentence: (1) the Senate vote count, (2) the appropriation amount and duration, (3) the framing claim that the bill will "permanently resolve" disputes.
Step 03
Priority
Each claim scored against the priority threshold. All three score above 30 and enter the verification queue.
Step 04
Verification
Vote count: confirmed by Senate.gov roll-call record and AP wire reporting. Independent sources. → Supported · +1.0
Appropriation: confirmed by the Congressional Budget Office summary and the bill text. → Supported · +1.0
"Permanently resolve" framing: water-rights legal scholars in the cited reporting describe the bill as a 7-year settlement, not a permanent one. → Overstated · −0.5
Result
Two supported claims, one overstated. Sources cited in the published report. The independence rule was applied at verification — the AP wire and Senate.gov are independent.
Article: 78/100
02
The skeptic asks
"What if the AI gets it wrong?"

Same prompt. Same standards. Every outlet.

Every verdict is generated automatically using the same prompt and the same standards, applied identically to every outlet. No human editorial decisions sit between an article and its verdict.

That means a verdict is reproducible: re-running the same article through the same methodology produces the same result. It also means there's no editor with a thumb on the scale for any outlet — favorable or unfavorable.

6-hour gate
Claims from articles less than 6 hours old don't yet affect outlet scores. They need time for corroborating sources to surface. The gate is enforced uniformly across every scoring surface — leaderboard, outlet pages, score history, article badges.
Opinion excluded
"Is this accurate?" is the wrong question for editorial commentary. Opinion content is excluded from outlet scoring — not because opinion is bad, but because reliability is the wrong axis to judge it on.
Not verifiable
Absence of evidence is not evidence of inaccuracy. When a claim cannot be confirmed or denied — sources unavailable, paywalled, empirically untestable — it's marked Not verifiable and excluded from scoring rather than counted against the outlet.
Wire reprints
Verbatim Reuters, AP, AFP, and Bloomberg reprints are removed entirely from the outlet's claim pool. An outlet cannot earn a high score by republishing wire copy.
Quoted speakers
When an outlet reports what a politician said, the verdict evaluates whether the outlet quoted the politician accurately — not whether the politician's underlying claim is true. The politician's claim is scored against the politician's record, not the outlet's.
03
The skeptic asks
"What's your political bias?"

The methodology doesn't know what political affiliation an outlet has.

The pipeline ingests an article, extracts factual claims, runs each claim against the same evidence-finding process, and assigns a verdict from the same eight categories. Every outlet runs the same code path. There is no dial labeled "left" or "right." There is no editor who decides which outlets get scrutiny.

What we never call ourselves.

Verum Signal never describes itself as a fact-checker. Fact-checking is a craft — one that involves human judgment, editorial framing, and choices about what to cover. Verum Signal does something narrower: it evaluates claims on evidence using a documented methodology, applied uniformly. We provide the signals. You decide.

What the leaderboard reflects.

Outlet scores are a function of the verdicts that outlet has accumulated under this methodology. They are not curated. They are not adjusted for editorial reasons. They change as more verdicts are accumulated, and the trajectory of any outlet's score is itself public.

The methodology is versioned. Changes are documented. Old versions are archived.

Methodology versions are bumped when scoring math, verdict definitions, or inclusion rules change. Substantive changes are documented. Archived versions remain available and continue to be browsable.

05
The skeptic asks
"What can't you do?"

The honest limitations of this approach.

A methodology should be honest about what it can't do. Some of what follows is by design; some is a function of where the system is in its lifecycle. Either way, it's worth saying out loud.

Limited outlet coverage today.

The leaderboard is still maturing. Outlets need at least 20 verdicts to receive a public score, and many outlets are below that threshold. Coverage will expand as the corpus grows.

Paywalled sources can limit verification.

When the only credible source for a claim sits behind a paywall the system cannot access, the claim may land in Not verifiable — excluded from scoring rather than counted against the outlet.

Articles missing publication timestamps are excluded.

A subset of ingested articles arrive without a parseable publication date and are excluded from outlet scoring under the date-required policy. The ingestion pipeline is being improved to reduce this exclusion rate.

Opinion content is not "judged."

Opinion is excluded from scoring rather than rated, because reliability is the wrong axis to judge editorial commentary on. This means an opinion-heavy outlet has fewer scoreable claims, not a lower score.

Verdict definitions

Eight possible verdicts.

Every claim lands in one of eight categories. Six contribute to the outlet's reliability score. Two (Opinion and Not verifiable) are excluded from scoring. The thresholds are fixed, not negotiable.

The number on each card is the verdict's weight in the outlet's reliability score. Positive weights push the score up; negative weights pull it down.
